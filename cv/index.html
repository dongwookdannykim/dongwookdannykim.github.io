Professional Summary

I study how states create, justify, and stabilize claims of right over legally open global commons. My dissertation and first-book project, “Authority without Territory,” asks why some such claims stabilize into accepted rights while others fail. My work draws on historical, legal, policy, and multi-method evidence.

Dissertation and Book Project

  • Authority without Territory: State Claims of Right in the Global Commons. Asks why some state attempts to establish sovereignty-related authority over legally open global commons stabilize as accepted claims of right, while others fail.
  • Argues such claims stabilize when three conditions interlock: accommodation by powers capable of disruption, resonance in legitimating vocabularies, and constructive ambiguity within a shared framework.
  • Empirical chapters examine the exclusive economic zone, air defense identification zones, the Australian Maritime Identification Zone, and the Bogota Declaration.
  • The book manuscript adds a polar/Arctic chapter extending the argument to climate-driven and infrastructural transformations of the global commons.
