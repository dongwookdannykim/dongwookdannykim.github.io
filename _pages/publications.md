My research examines how states create, justify, and stabilize claims of right over the global commons—spaces such as the high seas, international airspace, outer space, and the polar regions that lie beyond any single state’s territory. I am interested in how authority can take hold in places that no one owns, and why some of these claims come to be accepted as legitimate while others are resisted or collapse.

## Dissertation and first book project

My dissertation and first book project, *Authority without Territory: State Claims of Right in the Global Commons*, asks why some state attempts to establish sovereignty-related authority over legally open global commons stabilize as accepted claims of right, while others fail.

I develop a theory of *authority without territory*: arrangements in which states claim the right to regulate, identify, appropriate, or exclude within the global commons while stopping short of full territorial sovereignty and preserving the commons freedoms of others. I argue that such claims stabilize when three conditions interlock: accommodation by the powers capable of disrupting them, resonance within shared vocabularies of legitimacy, and a degree of constructive ambiguity that lets rival actors coexist under a common framework.

The empirical chapters trace this logic across several cases—the exclusive economic zone, air defense identification zones, the Australian Maritime Identification Zone, and the Bogotá Declaration—drawing on historical, legal, and policy evidence to explain why some claims consolidated into durable rights while others did not. The book manuscript extends the argument with a chapter on polar and Arctic governance, where climate change and new infrastructure are reshaping what authority over the commons can mean.

## Projects in development

Building on the dissertation, I am developing several related projects:

- **Polar and Arctic governance.** How climate change and infrastructural transformation are creating new occasions for states to claim rights over a rapidly changing commons.
- **Territorialization in the South China Sea.** How efforts to convert open maritime space into controlled territory test the limits of authority without territory.
- **Artificial islands in the Asia-Pacific.** How built features sit at the crossroads of geopolitics, capitalism, and the climate crisis, and what they reveal about sovereignty in contested waters.

My empirical work is supported by training in qualitative and multi-method research, geospatial analysis, and computational social science. A full list of working papers and presentations is available on my [CV](/cv/).
